# CBOW & SG - Definition & Overview

Imagine you have a **giant playground** where every word in the world has its own special spot to sit. **Word2Vec** is the system that decides exactly where each word's spot should be.

## 1. The Word Playground
In this playground, words that are "best friends" sit very close together. For example, **"Apple"** and **"Banana"** sit next to each other because they are both yummy fruits. But a word like **"Bicycle"** would sit far away in the toy section. Because they have "addresses" (which grown-ups call **vectors**), the computer can find them easily.

## 2. The Guessing Games
To learn where everyone should sit, the computer plays two games:

*   **Game 1: Fill-in-the-Blank (CBOW):** I show you a sentence like *"The [blank] barked at the mailman."* You look at the words around the blank and guess the word **"Dog"**.
*   **Game 2: Who are your neighbors? (Skip-gram):** I show you just one word, like **"Cat,"** and you have to guess what other words might be nearby, like **"Meow"** or **"Fur"**.

By playing these games billions of times, the computer gets really smart at knowing which words belong together.

## 3. Magic Word Math
Because every word has a spot on the playground map, you can do **magic math tricks**. If you start at the word **"King,"** take away his "boy-ness" (**Man**), and add "girl-ness" (**Woman**), the map points you directly to the word **"Queen"**!

## 4. Word Legos (The "FastText" Trick)
Sometimes, the computer sees a new word it has never heard before. To help, it looks at the word like it’s made of **Lego blocks**. If it sees the word **"Unlucky,"** it might not know that specific word, but it knows what **"un-"** means and what **"luck"** means from other words. It puts those pieces together to guess exactly where that new word should sit in the playground.

## What is CBOW & SG?
In the Word2Vec framework, **CBOW (Continuous Bag-of-Words)** and **SG (Skip-gram)** are the two primary log-linear model architectures used to learn distributed word representations from large datasets. While both are shallow neural networks with a single hidden layer, they differ fundamentally in their training objectives and how they process context.

### **Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW)**
*   **Core Logic:** The CBOW model is designed to **predict a target center word** based on the surrounding **context words**.
*   **The "Bag-of-Words" Aspect:** All input context word vectors are projected into the same position and **averaged** (or summed) to form the projection layer. Because the vectors are averaged, the **specific order of words in the context does not influence the prediction**, which is why it is referred to as a "bag-of-words" model.
*   **Efficiency:** CBOW is generally **faster to train** than Skip-gram and tends to perform slightly better on **syntactic tasks**.

### **Skip-gram (SG)**
*   **Core Logic:** The Skip-gram model works in the opposite direction; it uses a **single input word to predict the surrounding context words** within a specific range.
*   **Training Task:** The network is trained on pairs of words (word1, word2) generated by moving a window across the text. For every input word, the model attempts to maximize the classification of words within a certain distance.
*   **Distance and Sampling:** To account for the fact that distant words are usually less related to the center word, Skip-gram **samples less from distant words** during training. This is often achieved by randomly selecting a window size ($R$) for each training instance that is smaller than or equal to the maximum specified window size ($C$).
*   **Performance:** While more computationally expensive than CBOW, Skip-gram significantly outperforms it on **semantic relationship tasks**.

### **Comparison Summary**
| Feature | Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) | Skip-gram (SG) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Prediction Goal** | Predicts the **center word** from context. | Predicts the **context words** from the center. |
| **Input Handling** | **Averages** context word vectors. | Uses **individual word pairs**. |
| **Word Order** | Disregards word order in context. | Focuses on word-to-word relationships. |
| **Complexity** | Lower; faster training. | Higher; slower training. |
| **Best For** | **Syntactic tasks** (grammar/structure). | **Semantic tasks** (meaning/analogies). |

## Difference between CBOW & SG
The **Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW)** and **Skip-gram (SG)** models differ primarily in the direction of their prediction and how they handle the relationship between a word and its context during the training process,.

### 1. Training Direction and Task
*   **CBOW Logic:** The goal of CBOW is to **predict a target center word** based on its surrounding context words,. It takes the words from a specified window (both history and future) as input to a log-linear classifier,.
*   **Skip-gram Logic:** Conversely, Skip-gram predicts the **surrounding context words** given a single input center word,. It uses each current word as the input to maximize the classification of other words within a certain range before and after it,.

### 2. Context Representation
*   **CBOW (Averaging):** In CBOW, all context word vectors are projected into the same position and **averaged**,. Because it uses an average, the specific **order of words in the context does not influence the projection**, which is why it is referred to as a "bag-of-words" model,.
*   **Skip-gram (Pair-wise):** Skip-gram treats the relationship as a series of **individual word pairs** (word1, word2) generated by moving a window across the text,. It trains the network to give a predicted probability distribution of nearby words for that specific input word,.

### 3. Handling of Distance and Complexity
*   **Skip-gram Range Sampling:** Skip-gram often uses a maximum distance ($C$) to define the context. For each training word, it randomly selects a number ($R$) in the range of 1 to $C$ and then uses $R$ words from the history and $R$ words from the future as labels,. This requires $R \times 2$ word classifications for each input,. To account for the fact that distant words are usually less related, the model **samples less from distant words**,.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The complexity of CBOW is $Q = N \times D + D \times \log_2(V)$, where $N$ is the context size and $D$ is the vector dimensionality,. Skip-gram's complexity is $Q = C \times (D + D \times \log_2(V))$, making it more computationally expensive as the distance $C$ increases,.

### 4. Performance Differences
*   **CBOW** generally performs better on **syntactic tasks** and is faster to train because it aggregates context,.
*   **Skip-gram** is slightly worse on syntactic tasks but significantly outperforms CBOW on **semantic relationship tasks**,,. This makes Skip-gram particularly effective when trained on large datasets to discover subtle semantic similarities, such as relationships between cities and countries,.

## What does semantic relationship mean?
In the context of the sources provided, a **semantic relationship** refers to the underlying **meaning and conceptual similarity** shared between words, rather than just their alphabetical spelling or grammatical structure. 

Key aspects of semantic relationships in these models include:

*   **Vector Distance:** In a Word2Vec model, words with similar meanings are represented as **vectors that are close to each other** in a multi-dimensional space. For example, the words **"strong" and "powerful"** would be positioned near one another because they share a semantic connection, whereas "strong" and "Paris" would be distant.
*   **Linear Algebra and Analogies:** Semantic relationships are often demonstrated through **linear regularities**, where algebraic operations on word vectors can solve conceptual analogies. A famous example is `vec("king") - vec("man") + vec("woman")`, which results in a vector very close to **"queen"**. 
*   **Common Categories:** The sources identify several specific types of semantic relationships used to evaluate how well a model understands language, including:
    *   **Common Capital Cities:** Paris is to France as Tokyo is to Japan.
    *   **Family Members:** Brother is to sister as dad is to mom.
    *   **Currency:** Relating a country to its specific money, such as Kazakhstan to the tenge or Iran to the rial.
*   **Discovery through Context:** These relationships are learned automatically from large volumes of text by analyzing the **context** in which words appear. The **Skip-gram (SG) model** is noted for being particularly effective at capturing these complex semantic relationship tasks compared to other architectures.

Understanding these relationships allows computer models to perform advanced tasks such as **automatic text tagging, recommender systems, and machine translation** by moving beyond treating words as simple, meaningless indices.
